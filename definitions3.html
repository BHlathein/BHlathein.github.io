Definitions

1. Glaciation and Glacial Features

Milankovitch Cycles

Milankovitch cycles are long-term variations in Earth's orbit and axial tilt that affect climate patterns and contribute to glacial and interglacial periods.

Alpine Glacier

An alpine glacier is a type of glacier that forms in mountainous regions, moving through valleys and carving out distinct landforms.

Continental Ice Sheet

A continental ice sheet is a massive sheet of ice that covers large areas of continents, such as the Antarctic or Greenland ice sheets.

Zone of Accumulation

The zone of accumulation is the part of a glacier where snow and ice accumulate, leading to the glacier's growth.

Zone of Ablation

The zone of ablation is the part of a glacier where ice melts or calvings occur, leading to the glacier's shrinkage.

Roche Moutonnee

A roche moutonnee is a rock formation shaped by the passage of a glacier, characterized by a smooth, rounded side and a jagged, steep side.

Glacial Striations

Glacial striations are scratches or grooves found on rock surfaces, formed by the movement of rocks and debris beneath a glacier.

Horn

A horn is a sharp, pointed peak formed by the erosion of glaciers on multiple sides of a mountain.

Arête

An arête is a sharp ridge formed by the erosion of two glaciers on opposite sides of a mountain.

Cirque

A cirque is a bowl-shaped valley or amphitheater carved by a glacier at the head of a valley.

Hanging Valley

A hanging valley is a small valley that is elevated above the main valley, often formed by a tributary glacier.

Till

Till is unsorted glacial debris, consisting of a mix of sand, clay, gravel, and boulders, deposited directly by a glacier.

Glacial Erratic

A glacial erratic is a large rock that has been transported by a glacier, often found far from its source.

End Moraine

An end moraine is a ridge of debris deposited at the terminus of a glacier, marking its furthest advance.

Medial Moraine

A medial moraine is a ridge of debris formed at the junction of two glaciers, where their lateral moraines converge.

Lateral Moraine

A lateral moraine is a ridge of debris that forms along the sides of a glacier.

Outwash Deposit

An outwash deposit is a layer of sand and gravel laid down by meltwater flowing from a glacier.

Esker

An esker is a long, winding ridge of sand and gravel deposited by a stream flowing beneath a glacier.

Drumlin

A drumlin is a streamlined, elongated hill of glacial till formed by the flow of a glacier.

2. Seismic Terms

Focus

The focus, or hypocenter, is the point within the Earth where an earthquake originates.

Epicenter

The epicenter is the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus of an earthquake.

Scarp

A scarp is a steep slope or cliff formed by faulting or erosion.

Body Wave

Body waves are seismic waves that travel through the Earth's interior, including P-waves and S-waves.

P-Wave

A P-wave (primary wave) is a type of seismic wave that travels through solids, liquids, and gases, and is the fastest seismic wave.

S-Wave

An S-wave (secondary wave) is a type of seismic wave that travels through solids and is slower than a P-wave.

Surface Wave

Surface waves are seismic waves that travel along the Earth's surface, causing more damage than body waves.

Rayleigh Wave

A Rayleigh wave is a type of surface wave that moves in an elliptical motion, similar to ocean waves.

Love Wave

A Love wave is a type of surface wave that moves side to side, causing horizontal shaking.

Seismic Amplification

Seismic amplification refers to the increase in seismic wave intensity due to the local geological conditions, such as loose soil or soft rock.

Mercalli Intensity

The Mercalli intensity scale measures the intensity of an earthquake based on its observed effects on people, buildings, and the Earth's surface.

Richter Magnitude

The Richter magnitude scale measures the size of an earthquake based on the amplitude of seismic waves recorded by seismographs.

Seismograph

A seismograph is an instrument that records the intensity, duration, and other properties of seismic waves during an earthquake.

Stress

Stress is the force applied to a rock, causing deformation or strain.

Confining Stress

Confining stress is uniform pressure applied in all directions, such as the pressure from overlying rocks.

Directed Stress

Directed stress is stress that is applied in a particular direction, leading to deformation in that direction.

Compression

Compression is a type of directed stress that shortens a material, typically occurring at convergent plate boundaries.

Tension

Tension is a type of directed stress that stretches a material, typically occurring at divergent plate boundaries.

Shear

Shear stress occurs when forces are applied parallel to a material's surface, causing it to deform by sliding or twisting.

Strain

Strain is the deformation that results from applied stress.

Brittle

Brittle materials fracture or break under stress rather than deforming plastically.

Ductile

Ductile materials deform plastically under stress without breaking.

Joint

A joint is a fracture in rock where no movement has occurred.

Hanging Wall

The hanging wall is the block of rock above a fault plane, typically found in reverse or normal faults.

Footwall

The footwall is the block of rock below a fault plane, typically found in reverse or normal faults.

Normal Fault

A normal fault occurs when the hanging wall moves downward relative to the footwall, typically associated with tension.

Reverse Fault

A reverse fault occurs when the hanging wall moves upward relative to the footwall, typically associated with compression.

Thrust Fault

A thrust fault is a type of reverse fault with a low-angle fault plane.

Strike-Slip Fault

A strike-slip fault occurs when two blocks of rock move horizontally past each other.

3. Plate Tectonics and Geologic Features

Anticline

An anticline is a fold in rock layers that arches upward.

Syncline

A syncline is a fold in rock layers that sinks downward.

Monocline

A monocline is a bend in rock layers that results in a step-like structure.

Dome

A dome is a circular or oval-shaped uplift of rock layers, often caused by tectonic forces.

Basin

A basin is a large, bowl-shaped depression in the Earth's surface formed by tectonic processes.

Core

The core is the Earth's innermost layer, consisting of a solid inner core and a liquid outer core, primarily composed of iron and nickel.

Mantle

The mantle is the layer of the Earth between the crust and core, composed of semi-rigid silicate rock.

Oceanic Crust

The oceanic crust is the thinner, denser crust that forms the ocean floor, composed mainly of basalt.

Continental Crust

The continental crust is the thicker, less dense crust that forms the continents, composed mainly of granite.

Seismic Discontinuities

Seismic discontinuities are boundaries between different layers within the Earth, marked by changes in seismic wave speeds.

Convection

Convection is the movement of heat through a fluid (like the mantle), where hot material rises and cooler material sinks.

Lithosphere

The lithosphere is the rigid outer layer of the Earth, composed of the crust and the uppermost part of the mantle.

Asthenosphere

The asthenosphere is the semi-fluid layer beneath the lithosphere, allowing tectonic plates to move over it.

Continental Drift (Wegener)

Continental drift is the hypothesis proposed by Alfred Wegener that continents once formed a single supercontinent (Pangaea) and have drifted apart over time.

Pangea

Pangea is the name of the supercontinent that existed around 300 million years ago, where all the continents were joined together.

Mid-Ocean Ridge

A mid-ocean ridge is an underwater mountain range formed by divergent tectonic plates.

Magnetic Anomalies (on the Seafloor)

Magnetic anomalies on the seafloor are variations in Earth's magnetic field, caused by the alignment of magnetic minerals in rocks during seafloor spreading.

Magnetic Reversal

Magnetic reversal is the process where Earth's magnetic field flips, with the magnetic north and south poles switching places.

Geomagnetic Reversal Time Scale

The geomagnetic reversal time scale is a record of the history of magnetic reversals preserved in the Earth's rocks.

Seafloor Spreading

Seafloor spreading is the process by which new oceanic crust is formed at mid-ocean ridges and moves away from the ridge.

Lithospheric Plate

A lithospheric plate is a rigid segment of the Earth's lithosphere that moves and interacts with other plates at plate boundaries.

Active (Continental) Margin

An active continental margin is a tectonically active area where oceanic crust is subducting beneath continental crust.

Passive (Continental) Margin

A passive continental margin is a tectonically stable area where oceanic crust is not currently interacting with continental crust.

Divergent (Plate) Boundary

A divergent plate boundary is a boundary where two tectonic plates are moving away from each other.

Convergent (Plate) Boundary

A convergent plate boundary is a boundary where two tectonic plates are moving toward each other, leading to subduction or collision.

Transform (Plate) Boundary

A transform plate boundary is a boundary where two tectonic plates slide past each other horizontally.

Pillow Basalt

Pillow basalt is a type of volcanic rock formed when lava erupts underwater, creating rounded, pillow-like shapes.

Continental Rift

A continental rift is a tectonic boundary where a continent is being pulled apart, leading to the formation of a new ocean basin.

Subduction Zone

A subduction zone is an area where one tectonic plate is being forced beneath another, often leading to volcanic activity and earthquakes.

Trench

A trench is a deep, narrow depression in the Earth's crust formed by subduction zones.

Accretionary Prism

An accretionary prism is a wedge of sediment and rock scraped off the subducting plate and accumulated in the trench.

Forearc Basin

A forearc basin is a sedimentary basin located between an active volcanic arc and a subduction trench.

Volcanic Arc

A volcanic arc is a chain of volcanoes formed above a subduction zone.

Continental Margin Arc

A continental margin arc is a volcanic arc located along the edge of a continent, typically formed by oceanic-continental subduction.

Oceanic Island Arc

An oceanic island arc is a chain of volcanic islands formed by oceanic-oceanic subduction.

"Ring of Fire"

The "Ring of Fire" is a major area of the Pacific Ocean basin where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur due to tectonic plate boundaries.

Tectonic Suture

A tectonic suture is a boundary between two tectonic plates that were once joined together but have since been separated due to tectonic movement.

Oceanic Fracture Zone

An oceanic fracture zone is a fault zone found on the ocean floor, typically associated with transform plate boundaries.

Mantle Plume

A mantle plume is an upwelling of hot, buoyant material from the Earth's mantle, often leading to volcanic activity.

Hotspot

A hotspot is an area of the Earth's mantle where plumes of hot material rise, often resulting in volcanic activity independent of tectonic plate boundaries.
