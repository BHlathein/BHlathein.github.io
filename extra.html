Definitions

1. Geology and Energy

Coal

Origin: Coal forms from the accumulation of plant material in swampy environments over millions of years. Heat, pressure, and time transform peat into various grades of coal.

Types:

  • Peat: An early stage of coal formation; partially decayed plant material.
  • Lignite: A soft, brownish coal; considered low-grade.
  • Bituminous Coal: A dense, black coal; used as a fuel source.
  • Anthracite: The hardest form of coal with the highest carbon content; burns cleanly.

Reserves: Coal reserves are found worldwide, with significant deposits in the United States, China, and Australia.

Petroleum

Origin: Petroleum forms from the remains of marine organisms buried under sediment. Over millions of years, heat and pressure convert these remains into oil and gas.

Components:

  • Reservoir Rock: A porous rock that stores oil and gas.
  • Cap Rock: An impermeable layer that traps oil and gas below.
  • Hydrofracturing: A technique to extract gas and oil from tight rock formations by injecting high-pressure fluid.

2. Geology and Christianity

Positions on Creation

Young-Earth Creationism (YEC): The belief that the Earth is only thousands of years old, based on a literal interpretation of the Bible. YEC proponents interpret geological formations, such as the Grand Canyon, as results of the biblical flood.

Progressive Creationism: The belief that God created life in stages over millions of years, aligning with scientific evidence for an old Earth.

Theistic Evolution: The belief that God used evolution as a tool to create life.

Naturalistic Evolution: The belief that life evolved without divine intervention, based on natural processes.

Old Earth Evidence

  • Fossil succession shows a gradual change in species over time.
  • Radiometric dating provides accurate ages for rocks and fossils.
  • Geological features, such as sedimentary layers and varves, indicate a history spanning millions of years.

3. Definitions

  • Peat: Partially decayed plant material in wetlands; the precursor to coal.
  • Marine Transgression: A rise in sea level that causes the shoreline to move inland.
  • Kerogen: A solid organic material in sedimentary rocks, which is a precursor to oil and gas.
  • Eustacy: Global changes in sea level caused by variations in the amount of water in the oceans.
  • Varves: Layers of sediment or sedimentary rock deposited annually in a lake or marine environment.
