Introduction to Geology

  • What is geology? Geology is the study of Earth's physical structure, materials, processes, and history.
  • Physical geology: Focuses on understanding Earth's materials (minerals, rocks) and processes (volcanism, erosion).
  • Relation to other sciences: Geology integrates physics, chemistry, biology, and environmental science to study Earth's systems.

Science and the Scientific Method

  • What is science? Science is the systematic study of the natural world through observation and experimentation.
  • What isn't science? Science is not based on opinions, beliefs, or untestable claims.
  • Limits of science: Assumes natural laws are consistent and cannot address supernatural or ethical questions.
  • Uniformitarianism: The principle that current geologic processes operated similarly in the past, forming Earth's features over time.
  • Simplified scientific method: Problems include oversimplification; real science involves iteration, feedback, and unpredictability.

Minerals

  • What is a mineral? A naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and crystal structure.
  • Crystal structure and properties: The arrangement of atoms determines a mineral's external shape and physical properties (e.g., cleavage).
  • Polymorphs: Minerals with the same composition but different structures (e.g., diamond and graphite).
  • Mineral formation: Crystallization from magma, precipitation, biological activity, or chemical alteration.
  • Silicate minerals: The silicon-oxygen tetrahedron is the basic unit of silicate minerals, forming various crystal structures (e.g., single chains, sheets).
  • 8 important silicates: Includes quartz, feldspar, mica, and olivine. Mafic silicates (e.g., olivine) are denser and darker than felsic silicates (e.g., quartz).
  • Non-silicates: Carbonates (calcite), oxides, sulfates, sulfides, and halides are key non-silicate groups.

Rocks

  • What is a rock? A naturally occurring aggregate of minerals.
  • Three types of rocks:
    • Igneous: Formed from cooling magma or lava.
    • Sedimentary: Formed from lithified sediments.
    • Metamorphic: Formed from existing rocks subjected to heat and pressure.
  • Rock cycle: Interrelated processes of formation, destruction, and reformation of rocks.

Magmas and Intrusive Igneous Rocks

  • Components of magma: Melt, solid crystals, and dissolved gases.
  • Intrusive vs. Extrusive: Intrusive rocks have coarse grains; extrusive rocks are fine-grained due to rapid cooling.

Volcanoes and Extrusive Igneous Rocks

  • Eruptive behavior: Silica-rich magmas are more viscous and explosive.
  • Volcano types: Shield, cinder cone, stratovolcano, dome, and caldera.

Sedimentary Rocks

  • Categories: Clastic, chemical, and organic sedimentary rocks.
  • Structures: Cross-bedding, mudcracks, and ripple marks help interpret environments.

Metamorphism and Metamorphic Rocks

  • Protolith: Original rock before metamorphism.
  • Foliation: Texture caused by directed pressure during metamorphism.
  • Metamorphic isograds: Boundaries marking mineral changes due to pressure/temperature.
